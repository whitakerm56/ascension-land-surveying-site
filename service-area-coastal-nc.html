Land Surveying & Elevation Certificates for Coastal North Carolina

Coastal North Carolina presents a set of survey needs that differ meaningfully from the rest of the state. The combination of federal flood zone regulations, North Carolina's Coastal Area Management Act (CAMA), shoreline change, tidal wetlands, and the high real estate value of waterfront property creates a specific, consistent demand for survey services that require both NC licensure and familiarity with the coastal regulatory environment. Ascension Land Surveying holds NC PLS License #L-5008 and serves coastal NC as the easternmost region of our corridor, from the Outer Banks through Currituck, Dare, Hyde, Carteret, Onslow, and New Hanover Counties.

Elevation Certificates for Coastal NC Property

FEMA flood elevation certificates are one of the most common survey deliverables on the North Carolina coast. If your property is in a Special Flood Hazard Area (SFHA) — Zone A, AE, VE, or similar FEMA flood zone designations — your lender will require a current elevation certificate to determine flood insurance premiums under the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP). On the NC Outer Banks and throughout Dare, Carteret, and New Hanover Counties, the majority of waterfront and near-water residential and commercial properties fall into flood zones that require this documentation.

A flood elevation certificate must be completed by a licensed land surveyor, engineer, or architect, and it documents the structure's elevation relative to the Base Flood Elevation (BFE) established in FEMA's Flood Insurance Rate Maps. Accurate elevation certificates can result in significantly lower flood insurance premiums when a structure sits above the BFE. We complete elevation certificates for residential and commercial properties throughout coastal North Carolina.

Waterfront Boundary Surveys on the NC Coast

Waterfront boundary surveys on the North Carolina coast involve complexities that inland boundary work does not. The mean high water mark, which is the legal boundary line between private property and state-owned coastal water bottoms, shifts over time as shorelines erode, accrete, and migrate. On barrier islands like the Outer Banks, this movement is significant enough to affect property lines in ways that matter for permitting, construction setbacks, and title. Determining the true current location of the mean high water mark on an Outer Banks parcel requires fieldwork, tidal data, and experience with coastal boundary methodology, not just the application of inland survey techniques.

In addition to the mean high water line, coastal boundary surveys on the Outer Banks and elsewhere along the NC coast must contend with CAMA's estuarine shoreline setbacks, primary nursery area buffers, and development permit requirements. We flag these regulatory boundaries as part of our survey work on coastal properties.

Drone LiDAR for Low-Lying Coastal Terrain

The NC coast's flat, low-lying terrain is ideal for drone LiDAR work. Salt marshes, tidal flats, maritime shrub thickets, and dune systems that are difficult or unsafe to traverse on foot are efficiently captured in a drone LiDAR flight that produces accurate elevation data without requiring crews to enter wet or unstable terrain. For coastal developers, engineers planning stormwater management on low-gradient sites, and environmental consultants mapping wetland extents, drone LiDAR is often the most practical method for obtaining the elevation data needed.

NC Licensing and Coastal Recording

All survey work in North Carolina requires NC PLS licensure. Ascension Land Surveying holds NC PLS #L-5008. Plats record at the county Register of Deeds. Coastal development projects also typically require CAMA permits through the NC Division of Coastal Management, and our surveys document the information those permit applications require.
